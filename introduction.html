Vaccine Overload

Created by: Johnathan Hernandez
j.hernandez06tx@gmail.com

In the early years of a child's life, they are given a number of vaccines in order to protect against a number of dangerous infections. Children will get vaccinations for a number of different diseases from MMR (Measles, Mumps, and Rubella), Chickenpox, and Polio along with routine vaccinations for diseases like the flu. A common concerns parents have is that a child's immune system will be overwhelmed by the number of vaccines they take, but evidence shows that immune systems are more than capable of handling the amount of vaccines a child takes.

Concerned parents may claim that too many vaccines in short succession will "overwhelm" or weaken their child's immune system. Such vaccination concerns have existed for a long time, with concerns regarding vaccines typically being overexaggerated, risking the lives of many who may be at risk1. However, it is still a concern that deserves time to be explored, since children are typically given a large number of vaccinations.

Vaccines work by introducing modified variants of pathogenic diseases or simply their antigens. The body's immune system uses B cells that are able to modify and generate different antigen receptors through V(D)J recombination, allowing for nearly limitless amounts of potential antigen binding sites2.

Because the body's immune system is able to recognize and fight a massive number of different infections at the same time, there is little risk in multiple vaccines in quick succession. Most vaccines contain far fewer than 100 antigens, with some only containing only a single antigen. Thanks to the advancements in vaccines, there are far fewer antigens in modern vaccines than in past vaccines. Each infant is possibly able to take 10,000 vaccines at the same time before the immune system is "overwhelmed"3.
